# ransom-core
### Recognizing Affect through Narrative, Semantics, and Observable Markers
 
*An open foundation for emotional recognition in artificial intelligence systems.*
 
*Named in honour of Elwin Ransom — C.S. Lewis's philologist who crossed worlds to understand forms of consciousness radically unlike his own.*
 
---
 
## The problem no one has solved
 
AI systems do not fail to understand human emotion because they lack processing power.
 
They fail because no one has given them the right data.
 
Current emotional datasets are built on labelled sentences, acted recordings, and flat taxonomies. A sentence tagged "anger." A face tagged "sadness." A voice clip tagged "fear."
 
This is not how emotion works.
 
Emotion is not a label. It is a configuration — a pattern that unfolds across time, across relationship, across context. It has texture, contradiction, and ambiguity. It masks itself. It transforms mid-expression. It means different things depending on who is speaking, to whom, in which cultural and relational moment.
 
No existing dataset captures this. And until someone builds one that does, AI systems will continue to misread the most important signals human beings produce.
 
---
 
## The hypothesis
 
Human beings have been documenting emotional complexity for thousands of years — not in datasets, but in art.
 
Literature. Cinema. Theatre. Painting. Music.
 
A novel by Dostoevsky does not tag "shame." It shows shame as a total configuration — the way it distorts perception, reshapes language, controls posture, generates specific defensive behaviors that look like arrogance, pride, or coldness to outside observers.
 
A film by Bergman does not classify grief. It unfolds grief across time — showing how it looks like anger for weeks before revealing itself, how it inhabits silence differently than any other state, how it changes the way a person occupies a room.
 
This is annotated data. Annotated not by a crowdsourcing platform but by writers, directors, actors, and critics — human experts in emotional recognition — over a century of intentional cultural production.
 
RANSOM is built on this hypothesis: **the archive already exists. We need to learn to read it.**
 
---
 
## What RANSOM is
 
RANSOM is an open research foundation with three components:
 
**1. A taxonomy of emotional configurations**
Not a list of emotion labels. A structured library of observable patterns — linguistic, prosodic, behavioral, relational — that constitute recognizable emotional states across cultural contexts.
 
Each configuration is defined not by its name but by its observable markers: what it sounds like, what it looks like in language, how it evolves over time, what it masks, what it reveals.
 
**2. An annotation methodology**
A rigorous protocol for extracting emotional configurations from cultural sources — film, literature, theatre — using trained human annotators: psychologists, actors, directors, writers.
 
The methodology distinguishes between universal configurations and culturally specific ones. It treats ambiguity as a valid state, not an annotation error.
 
**3. An open corpus**
The resulting dataset — configurations extracted, annotated, validated — released under open license for research, training, and application development worldwide.
 
---
 
## What RANSOM is not
 
RANSOM is not a product. It is infrastructure.
 
It is not a claim that AI can *understand* emotion. Recognition precedes understanding. We solve recognition first.
 
It is not a proprietary asset. The knowledge of how human beings feel belongs to humanity. A closed corpus of emotional truth is a capture, not an innovation.
 
---
 
## Why open
 
Peter Thiel built a philosophy of technology around the monopoly. In *Zero to One*, he argues that competition is pathology, that advantage must be defended, that the goal of innovation is to own a secret no one else has.
 
This project is a direct refusal of that philosophy.
 
C.S. Lewis — in *The Abolition of Man*, written in 1943 — described what happens when human beings abandon the capacity to recognize objective emotional value. He called it the rise of *the Conditioners*: those who, having discarded shared human feeling as a guide, use technique to shape others according to private will.
 
A closed corpus of emotional recognition would be exactly that. A small group deciding, privately, what human emotion means — and encoding that decision into systems that affect billions of people.
 
RANSOM is open because the alternative is worse than having no corpus at all.
 
The foundation is shared. The applications built on it are yours to own.

## How to contribute
 
We are looking for:
 
**Researchers** in affective computing, psychology, linguistics, and cognitive science — to validate and extend the taxonomy.
 
**Annotators** with backgrounds in psychology, acting, directing, or literary criticism — to extract configurations from cultural sources.
 
**Engineers** with experience in NLP, speech processing, and vector embeddings — to build the technical infrastructure.
 
**Cultural experts** — specialists in non-Western cinematic and literary traditions — to ensure the corpus is not another anglophone dataset wearing a universal mask.
 
If you are any of these things, open an issue or write to us directly.
